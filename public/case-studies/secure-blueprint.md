# Secure Blueprint
**Category:** Product Design — Agency

---

## Case Study

Chief information security officers have long operated in a discipline where readiness is inherently subjective — there are things you can do, but how prepared you actually are is hard to quantify. NIST, a federal standards framework introduced in the late '90s, attempted to address this with a long series of questions designed to gauge system readiness. The problem was that the raw output wasn't actionable: answers didn't clearly indicate what they meant or what to prioritize, and compiling the results into a leadership-ready report took the better part of a quarter to execute — leaving little time to actually act on it.

Our clients had already done meaningful work to address this, recategorizing NIST's questions into a Likert-scale format so teams could more quickly see what needed attention versus what was working. What they needed was a platform to operationalize it.

We built a system where CISOs could define their team and assign ownership of specific question sets to the right people, reducing and simplifying the total questions someone had to answer on a recurring cycle. Goals could be set and tracked over time — where the team started, where they are now, where they're headed. Roll-up views let leadership get a fast read on system health at both category and detailed levels. A built-in reporting tool let CISOs quickly structure presentations so specific answers could become leadership-ready slide readouts.

I partnered with a product strategist, leading a significant portion of the design strategy and contributing meaningfully to product strategy. The project ran on a multi-week client collaboration cycle — roughly two weeks of concept, two weeks of testing, and a final week of revisions.

This moved CISOs from a once-a-quarter, manual compile-and-guess process to continuous, lightweight check-ins on specific pieces of their system — with recommendations generated and team confidence re-measured as things improved. The platform was adopted by a number of Fortune 500 C-suites and was later acquired by a major cybersecurity organization.

## Raw Notes (verbatim)

Secure Blueprint was developed for a set of clients who were chief information security officers and other security professionals. The challenge with security is that it's subjective. Yes there are things you can do. There are tactical things you could do but the readiness is very subjective, based on a number of things and how people feel about it.

To solve this issue and ensure that the nation's products are secure, the national government introduced a set of standards in the late 90s called NIST. These were a long series of questions that you could have your security professionals answer that would gauge the readiness of your system and also other things that you should do, like things that you should focus on. Basically it wasn't a very clear way to do this. It was just like a long list. Up questions. A lot of a CISO's job would be to query their team and then compile this information and then present a report to their leadership about what they should be investing in. This should be done quarterly but it would take a whole quarter to even run this. The data output wasn't very structured in the sense that it wasn't very clear what certain answers meant. You should work on this. It was a lot of guesswork and a lot of fuzziness, which is not stuff that security professionals like. And so what our clients had done was take this work, the set of questions, and categorize them so that you knew what you should work on based on a series of questions. Those questions were all Likert scale and helped teams quickly understand what you needed to focus on and what was doing well with this system.

We worked on a system for communicating all that. We developed a platform where chief information security officers can set up their team and determine what those team members were in charge of, basically who should you ask what questions to. We were able to simplify the number of questions someone has to answer. We developed a recurring cycle of questions. We developed a feature where they could set goals and track where they started, where they were, and where they were going. We presented a number of views that let them at a high glance mostly how they were doing at a category level and at a specific area level. We built out a way for them to quickly structure reports and slides so that they could quickly ask questions and get specific answers and slide readouts to their leadership. This moved the needle from them spending an entire quarter compiling all the stuff, asking these questions, and guessing. To be able to constantly be just checking in and querying different little pieces of their system. It would produce recommendations and then you could check your team's confidence on how the needle moved based on asking them the series of questions again. This product was rolled out to a number of Fortune 500 C-suites and was later acquired by a large cyber security organization.

Oh I partnered with a product strategist on this and led a large portion of the design strategy and some of the product strategy. The collaboration was with the clients, with multi-week check-ins, and I think the project probably took three weeks: two initially on concept, two weeks of testing, and a final week of revisions.
