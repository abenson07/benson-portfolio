# Contextual Messaging
**Category:** Product Design — Employer: Meta

---

## Case Study

Web Signals accounted for 97% of Facebook's advertising revenue. For advertisers, setting those signals up correctly — and staying compliant with new regulations — was an ongoing obligation. But the primary mechanism for communicating with them inside the platform had broken down.

Twelve teams across Facebook all shipped into the same surface, each trying to drive their own metrics through guidance cards — the one available tool. With no prioritization system, dozens competed for the same space at once. The platform had grown to over 200 possible notifications, engagement was effectively zero, and the signal-to-noise ratio had made the whole system easy to ignore. I was tasked with fixing it.

Working with a junior designer I was mentoring at the time, I introduced a new messaging surface within Setup Quality and reorganized everything into three distinct types: inline hints tied directly to the element they applied to, higher-level recommendations scoped to a specific quality metric, and a dismissible "What's New" card summarizing recent updates. This collapsed a sprawling, competing notification system into one coherent, scalable framework that other teams could build into.

Notification volume dropped sharply. Engagement climbed significantly. People found the system more focused and actually started acting on the recommendations and compliance updates being surfaced.

## Raw Notes (verbatim)

During my time at Facebook I was on the Web Signals team and Web Signals accounted for a portion of Facebook's revenue, 97%. A lot of my work there was focused on building experiences that help people set up their Web Signals properly and validate that work but there's also a lot of trying to get them to adopt best practices or make changes, especially with new regulations. There's a lot of things they needed to follow for compliance now.

If you want to picture this for a second, 12 teams across Facebook all shipping into this platform, trying to get them to do what they wanted them to do to move their own teams' metrics. Recommendation bars, or as we called them guidance cards, had become the only way of doing this and users were not adopting them. Engagement was non-existent and this is because there were dozens of them. They competed for what could be shown at a time and so they weren't very good. We accounted for over 200 notifications and I was tasked with simplifying this and improving this.

I worked with a junior designer who I was mentoring at the time. What we did was introduce a new surface on set up quality, which is a separate project but in set up quality we used that system and distilled down the number of notifications into three types:
- Hints or suggestions needed to be in line with the surface they were on, like a specific table row or something.
- Recommendations for a specific type of set up quality metric that they could adopt. These were higher level, basically the guidance cards.
- What we called a "What's new" card, which summarized a number of things that were new and were dismissible.

By doing this we reduced the number of notifications that a person was receiving by more than, I don't remember, some crazy number, a lot. We were able to distill all this down into a scalable platform and framework that people could use. Engagement shot up. People found it much more useful and focused and actually took actions on the recommendations and best practices that we were telling them to take.
