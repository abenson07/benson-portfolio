# Boring App
**Category:** Product Design — Employer: Crema (speculative)

---

## Case Study

This wasn't a client project. It started as a skill-building exercise during Crema's Lab Fridays — a Google 20%-style initiative the agency extended to cover tool exploration and individual skill development. InVision Studio had just launched, bringing timeline-based animation and tweening into screen design for the first time. Those capabilities would later become core to how Figma worked, and I wanted to get ahead of them.

I don't learn tools in isolation. I need a real problem. Elon Musk's Boring Company had just been announced — tunnel-based transit focused on Los Angeles, built around Tesla integration — and all that existed publicly were a couple of promo images. That was enough to start.

What began as a few screens turned into a full transit system concept. The underlying design problem was real: how do you accommodate people with different priorities — cost, speed, directness — within a single underground network? I built the solution around three transit modes borrowed from the existing world: a train-style fixed route with no deviation, a bus-style shared vehicle that could carry more people, and a carpool model where riders heading the same direction share a car that adjusts along the way. For anyone who needed to move fast and could pay for it, a Tesla pickup was available directly — accessed via street-level elevators leading into the tunnel network. The app communicated trade-offs between each option clearly across speed, time, and cost, and added utility along the ride — entertainment, food pickup along the route.

The project also solved a separate problem: most of my agency work at the time was under NDA. This gave me something real to put in a portfolio.

The app was never built, but the work found an audience. A Swiss drone manufacturer requested permission to adapt the interface for their controllers. A philanthropist reached out about using it as an interactive aviation education tool for kids. Neither went further — but both were unsolicited.

At the time Elon Musk's Boring Company had just kind of been announced and it was really interesting trying to solve traffic and infrastructure and urban growth and all of that stuff.
