# Flight Pro
**Category:** Product Design — speculative

---

## Case Study

The problem with working in an agency and being under an NDA means that your portfolio starts to become pretty lacking. During 2018, I needed to come up with something substantial to put into my portfolio. 

Around that time, I had recently taken my first intro flight for pilot lessons. I had been enamored with flying since I was a child and had played with Flight Simulator on an old Windows 95 computer. That introductory flight lesson at 28 in a Cessna 152 had me taking off, landing, maneuvering, and talking to air traffic control - in a real plane! Being in the cockpit for real made one thing immediately clear: general aviation aircraft were full of aging analog instrumentation, while touchscreens were just becoming genuinely capable in cars, and ADS-B Out was becoming a new FAA requirement at the same time.

The initial concept was a retrofit — a Tesla-style center console that could sit between pilot and co-pilot and replace a portion of the instrument panel, while leaving safety-critical analog dials in place. A mentor pushed further: it was just a case study, so why not explore the full version? That became the real project — a ground-up reimagining of a glass cockpit, where every design decision was driven not just by how something looked but by what it actually needed to do. Placement, size, and hierarchy all followed function.

Within that, several adjacent problems were worth solving: ATC radio is garbled and hard to parse in crowded airspace — voice-to-text addressed that. ADS-B out enabled real-time awareness of surrounding aircraft, not just on a flat map but in three dimensions, accounting for what was above and below. A landing assistance layer provided glide path guidance similar to what airports already use for large aircraft — extended to any pilot. Layers of map data, building on approaches Garmin had already introduced, were woven into the system. And for the most critical data — altitude, speed, distance to destination — a heads-up visor display kept it in the pilot's direct line of sight, borrowed from how large commercial airliners handle information hierarchy. The system was also designed to mirror and expand for a co-pilot seat, and to support dynamic camera access toggled on and off during flight. Select elements were taken into 3D modeling and I even explored integrating it into actual flight simulator software.


In the years since, this has continued to be a fan favorite: compliments during interviews, a Swiss drone manufacturer reaching out to use the interface for their flight controllers, and a philanthropist wanting to adapt it as an interactive aviation education tool for kids. But I still I want to fly it in my flight simulator, so if anyone wants to partner with me on that, hit me up.

## Raw Notes (verbatim)

Okay so this was another one of those speculative projects for my early portfolio. I was trying to intern and again worked at an agency where I couldn't share my work, very possibly. I needed to come up with some ideas for some projects I could design on the side. I didn't want them to be your normal boring, I don't know, Claude. You give me a couple of examples of your "basic designer portfolio thing" that I can point out. I didn't want to do this basic except my broken top.

Anyway all goes back to when I was really young. At 5 I got my hands on Microsoft Flight Simulator for the first time. Maybe not 5. Maybe I was like 11 and it was Microsoft Flight Simulator 95. I then progressed into some of the other flight simulators that they produced. I've always been fascinated with flying with just that software specifically and the idea of flying. I suck at math and was not a good student so I didn't really go into aviation. When I was 28 my partner at the time surprised me with a flight introductory lesson where I got to go up in a Cessna 152 and got to fly for an hour. I got to land the plane myself, take off myself, maneuver, and talk to aircraft control. It was really exciting and I felt somewhat comfortable because I had flown in that plane in a model so many times. But being in the cockpit was very evident that this was a very much older plane and a lot of times all of the dials and everything were analog dials. I was interested in solving, exploring that problem. Tesla was really big at the time. Touch screens in cars were just becoming a thing, like big ones basically, and I know there are a bunch of problems in aviation that could be solved with some technology. This is just as ADS-B out was becoming required at the time. I luckily had a couple of friends who were getting their general aviation license so I could talk to them and interview them and prototype a bunch of ideas, which became the first concept that I put together. The idea was a Tesla-style center console that you could place in these cockpits in between the pilot and the co-pilot. That would replace a lot of these things.

As I was playing with that, I was struggling with some of the questions around how you have backup real dials and physical dials because you can't have this stuff fail in the air. I was really trying to stay within those things. It was like how do you adapt these existing cockpits and also how do you do this in a way that they can still have these other things? And after I explored that pretty thoroughly, my mentor at the time pushed me and said, "It's just a case study. Why not just push it? What if it was all cockpit glass cockpits?" So I explored that and came up with this holistic end-to-end experience that redesigned all of the common dials in a way that was clean and understandable but also recognizable and actually worked. I didn't just focus on the visual design. I focused on also what needed to work. That influenced the placement, the size, and everything but I also solved some other problems. I knew that listening to air traffic control was confusing. There was a lot of garbled noise. You had to listen. It wasn't always clear what they were saying. You had to ask them to repeat. In crowded airspace that became even worse. That's one.

Voice-to-text was one thing that I thought through. I also thought through ADS-B out. That meant that you could now see what the other aircraft were, not just on a 2D surface but also in relation to above and below you and more information as well. It was provided in the cockpit as well, and I explored layers of maps, which were already something that Garmin had already introduced and a couple of other tools had integrated into the system.

I also thought through landing assistance: glide paths that you could track, similar to how airports use lights and audio systems to guide large planes into large airports. This would be available to everyone. I also got into the world of AR, exploring what if you could display this information directly in their view then I'd have to look down to look to see what's happening. With this I also thought through the fact that there's a lot going on here. What are the main things I need to know? Do I really need to look down and locate that on a complicated panel?

I borrowed some ideas from large airliners where I introduced a visor that had your main pieces of information:
- your altitude
- your speed
- your distance to your destination

all that stuff into a glanceable system that's right in front of you.

Lastly I thought about how this stuff needed to be reversed for systems that had a co-pilot but also could be expanded and adapted on top of it. I introduced cameras, but more specifically a way to access those cameras dynamically so you could turn them on and off and see them, similar to how some of the bar systems work. Lastly I tried to get into 3D modeling some of the stuff to put it actually into flight simulators. As I said this was not a real project but it did land me two jobs for being in a portfolio. I was approached by a drone manufacturer in Switzerland for permission to use the interface for their controllers. I also have to look up the name of the guy, a wealthy person philanthropist, who wanted to have me help them use this to teach kids about aviation and use it as an interactive tool that they could build into their systems. None of those things went anywhere but still cool.
