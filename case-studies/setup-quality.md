# Setup Quality
**Category:** Product Design — Employer: Meta

---

## Case Study

Confirming that a Pixel event fired was only the beginning. Whether that event was actually set up well — whether the data it produced was high quality or not — was a separate, more complex question, made up of several distinct signals. That information had no home. The existing UI was already overloaded, and the depth of data involved couldn't simply be added on top.

I worked directly with engineers to understand each individual quality signal, then helped categorize them into groupings that made logical sense from an information-architecture standpoint. One of the more important contributions in that process was pushing back on the engineers themselves: rather than giving qualified, hedged answers about whether a given signal was good or bad, I pushed for a clear binary determination that could actually be designed around and trusted by the people using it.

The solution was a bottom sheet pattern — a new layer that surfaced the full depth of information in context, without pulling the user off the page. Speed of navigation mattered; people were moving in and out of these views constantly, and the design had to accommodate that rhythm.

The result became a widely-used toolkit for engineers and marketers implementing server-side ad tracking — the modern approach that replaced client-side-only implementations after the privacy and regulatory changes that reshaped the ad industry.

## Raw Notes (verbatim)

So for setup quality, take a lot of the information at the beginning of the contextual messaging one. One of the main issues was that this is comparing information that was on top of information you were already presenting to them about setting their setup quality. This did the event fire and blew it up to several different aspects of what made an event high quality or low quality. We couldn't easily fit this into the existing UI because it was so much information and it was just an overused space. I worked with engineers directly to understand each individual piece, help categorize those different pieces into the correct kind of groupings that made sense, thought about the information architecture, and led the design of a new bottom sheet approach where you could basically expose more information without leaving the page. This is because they needed to hop in and out of these spaces so we needed to quickly see those things. There's a lot of information and a lot of complex information architecture data that needed to be communicated. You need to develop a new concept of what was considered good versus bad. I pushed engineers to quit equivocating and just say, "Is this good? Is this bad?" versus "It depends." This went through many rounds of review with multiple teams and different parts of the ad organization and ultimately was a big success and a massive toolkit for engineers and marketers implementing the server-first ad implementations of the modern era.
