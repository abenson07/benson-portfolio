# Eclipse RX
**Category:** Product Design — Agency

---

## Case Study

A dermatologist, tired of watching patients walk in overexposed, partnered with a physical product design firm to build a wearable — worn on the wrist or clipped to a bag — that could track UV exposure in real time. We were brought on to design the companion app that would make that data mean something.

There was no existing playbook. We built the underlying exposure metric ourselves, pulling from public data and scientific journals, then designed around two real constraints: the UI needed to communicate several live variables at once — current exposure, current UV rate, exposure accumulated through the day, and what to do about it — and the metric itself needed to behave intelligently, slowing its rate when someone took a real action like putting on a hat or applying sunscreen, rather than just climbing. An onboarding flow captured skin type up front so exposure tracking stayed accurate per person.

I led product and UI design, working alongside a product strategist focused on market positioning and an engineer building the underlying system. I partnered directly with the engineer on the logic itself — how exposure-slowing actions should affect the rate, and the reminder system prompting sunscreen reapplication.

The app shipped on the first wearable devices and was featured in Forbes. The product has since evolved into an Apple wearable ecosystem, still helping people stay safe in the sun today.

## Raw Notes (verbatim)

So let's change from the brief being what was broken or missing to what problem was this solving. It can be what's broken or missing or what was the problem the client had because some of these are agency.

For Eclipse RX the client was a dermatologist and was tired of seeing their patients coming in with over-exposure. This was when wearables were starting to become a thing, and they were working with a physical product design company to develop a wearable that their customers could wear on their wrist or clip on their clothing or their bag. They needed a companion app for the interface to let users know what their exposure was so we worked on that. Some of the challenges we needed to tackle were:
- finding a UI that clearly communicated a number of things: the current exposure, the current UV rate, their exposure throughout the day, their rate of exposure, and tracking actions that would slow the rate of their exposure
- there's also an onboarding flow that got a sense of their skin type to accurately assess the rate of exposure

This was intellectual property that we developed for them so they did not come in with a plan. We worked out using some data in the world and scientific journals and stuff to come up with some sort of reasonable metric for exposure. We also couldn't just continually add. If they were taking actions like wearing a shirt (like a long-sleeve shirt), a hat, or applying sunscreen, then we needed to slow the rate of exposure by a reasonable amount. We also needed to use visual indicators to warn them when exposure was too much or the UV was really high. I was mainly on the design of the product. We had a product strategist who was really focused on market positioning and collaboration with the materials people on the physical product design. We also had an engineer who was building the code and would take the data from the machine and track exposure over time. One thing I particularly assisted in was working with the developer to think through some logic chains for how we could slow the rate of exposure and how we could provide reminders and notifications and timers for re-application of sunscreen. The app was built and deployed on those initial devices. It was featured in Forbes, and the design and the product went on to be an Apple Watch strap that, as far as I know, still uses the same visuals.
